Multifunctional Hygiene & Comfort Pack

A socially responsible, dignity-focused redesign of what a “box” can be.

Summary

Our GMP1 project explored how a simple object like a box could be reimagined to address overlooked needs within homeless and low-income communities. We designed a soft, fabric-based hygiene and comfort pack inspired by an unfolded box layout. The pack stores menstrual and self-care items, includes hidden pockets for privacy, and unfolds into a small pillow that provides comfort for rest. By challenging the traditional idea of a rigid box, our project emphasized dignity, security, and mobility for vulnerable users. The prototype also highlighted how material choices and emotional considerations can shape meaningful, socially responsible design.

Design Problem

Homeless and low-income women often lack reliable access to menstrual products, self-care items, and any form of comfort during day-to-day life. Existing support systems prioritize food and shelter while overlooking hygiene and emotional needs that affect confidence, well-being, and physical health. Our challenge was to design an object that addressed both practical and emotional dimensions of care. We needed a solution that was portable, discreet, and user-friendly, while offering storage, comfort, and flexibility. The deeper problem became understanding how everyday objects could support dignity rather than simply provide function.

Design Process

Our design process began by grounding ourselves in the social realities of menstrual insecurity and homelessness. We analyzed how a box could be reframed as a system rather than a container, leading us to explore soft, foldable materials. From there, we sketched layouts based on an unfolded box net and translated them into fabric prototypes. One of our biggest challenges was technical: none of us knew how to sew, so we learned basic sewing techniques while constructing the prototype. Installing metal rods for structure proved physically demanding, and designing interior pockets required balancing storage capacity with portability.

Iteration was central to the project. Every time we realized that a certain pocket would too bulky or a strap wouldn't hold, we returned to quick sketches and brainstorming ways to alter our prototype without compromising on the vision, which was a process that reflected McGlashan’s idea of “asking questions of the artifact.” Sustainability also shaped our decisions. We sourced second-hand dresses, reclaimed stuffing, and reused materials to keep the design affordable and environmentally responsible. Throughout the process, Ratto’s concept of critical making guided our mindset that the prototype didn’t need to be perfect, but it needed to help us think deeply about how design can respond to lived experience.

Evaluation

Our final prototype demonstrated how reframing a familiar form can create new meaning and purpose. The hygiene and comfort pack challenged assumptions about what vulnerable populations need by valuing emotional well-being as much as practicality. Audience feedback during our presentation highlighted interest in the pack’s portability, comfort features, and potential for customization through community workshops. While the build was imperfect, the project successfully merged social awareness with hands-on experimentation. Ultimately, GMP1 strengthened my understanding of critical making, sustainability, and human-centered design as interconnected practices.
